Abstract
Breast cancer is one of the most prevalent and heterogeneous diseases worldwide, requiring
constant efforts in research and clinical advancements. Recent advancements in molecular
biology have enabled researchers to study gene expression profiles, which are crucial in
understanding the behavior of cancerous tissues and identifying molecular subtypes of breast
cancer. In this study, we aim to employ a range of advanced data science techniques such as
Principal Component Analysis (PCA), KMeans clustering, Topological Data Analysis (TDA),
Hierarchical Clustering, and Correlation Matrix Analysis to explore breast cancer gene
expression data. Through the application of these techniques, we aim to uncover patterns,
subtypes, and gene co-expression networks that can help provide a deeper understanding of
breast cancer progression. Our findings contribute to precision oncology by offering insights into
the molecular subtypes of breast cancer and the key gene networks involved, potentially guiding
the development of more targeted therapies. This study is an important step toward a more
comprehensive understanding of the molecular landscape of breast cancer.
1. Introduction
Breast cancer accounts for approximately 30% of all new cancer diagnoses in women
(American Cancer Society, 2023), making it a significant public health issue. Understanding
breast cancers molecular diversity is crucial for improving diagnosis, treatment, and patient
outcomes. Recent advances in gene expression profiling have provided a way to classify breast
cancer into molecular subtypes based on distinct gene expression patterns. These subtypes are
important for understanding tumor behavior, prognosis, and responses to specific therapies.
However, the complexity of gene expression data poses challenges. Each patient sample
typically contains thousands of genes, resulting in large, high-dimensional datasets. Extracting
meaningful insights from this data requires sophisticated mathematical techniques that reduce
dimensionality while preserving important patterns. These techniques allow researchers to
categorize patients into distinct subgroups based on their gene expression profiles, which can
lead to more personalized treatment strategies.
In this study, we employ several data science techniques to analyze a breast cancer gene
expression dataset, including PCA, KMeans clustering, Hierarchical Clustering, TDA, and
Correlation Matrix Analysis. By combining these methods, we aim to uncover hidden structures
in the data and identify molecular subtypes of breast cancer that may have biological and
clinical relevance.
2. Methods
2.1 Data Source and Preprocessing
The breast cancer gene expression data used in this study were obtained from the UCI Machine
Learning Repository, which is a rich source of high-quality datasets. The dataset contains
thousands of genes' expression levels for each patient sample, making it a high-dimensional
dataset. Before applying the analysis techniques, we performed data preprocessing steps,
which included data normalization using log transformations and z-score normalization. This
step ensures that differences in expression scales between genes do not introduce biases
during analysis.
We also filtered out genes with low variance across samples, as these genes are unlikely to
provide meaningful information for identifying cancer subtypes. By focusing on genes with high
variability, we increase the likelihood of detecting patterns related to breast cancer progression.
This preprocessing step is crucial for ensuring that the data is both clean and comparable
across different patient samples, which is essential for the reliability of downstream analyses.
2.2 Principal Component Analysis (PCA)
Principal Component Analysis (PCA) is a statistical technique that reduces the dimensionality of
large datasets while retaining the maximum amount of variance. In this study, we applied PCA
to project the high-dimensional gene expression data into a two-dimensional space, where each
point corresponds to a patient sample.
By projecting the data onto the first two principal components, which account for the most
variance in the dataset, we are able to visualize the overall structure of the data and observe
patterns of similarity and difference between samples. The results of PCA are visualized in
Figure 1, where each dot represents a patient sample, and the proximity between dots reflects
the similarity of their gene expression profiles. This dimensionality reduction helps us focus on
the most important features of the data without being overwhelmed by its complexity.
[PCA Visualization]
2.3 KMeans Clustering
KMeans clustering is a popular unsupervised machine learning algorithm used to partition data
into distinct clusters based on their similarities. In this study, KMeans clustering was applied to
the PCA-reduced data to identify potential subtypes of breast cancer. The algorithm iteratively
assigns each data point (patient sample) to the nearest cluster centroid and updates the
centroids based on the mean of the points in each cluster. This process continues until the
cluster assignments stabilize.
We experimentally determined that three clusters provided the best separation of the data, as
visualized in Figure 2. Each color in the figure represents a different cluster, and the samples
within each cluster share similar gene expression profiles. These clusters may correspond to
distinct molecular subtypes of breast cancer, each associated with different clinical outcomes
and responses to therapy.
[KMeans Clustering]
2.4 Hierarchical Clustering
While KMeans clustering provides a fixed number of clusters, Hierarchical Clustering builds a
tree-like structure (dendrogram) that shows how data points are grouped at varying levels of
similarity. This method does not require the specification of the number of clusters in advance.
Instead, it constructs a hierarchy of clusters, allowing us to explore the relationships between
samples at multiple distance thresholds.
Figure 3 shows the hierarchical clustering dendrogram for the breast cancer gene expression
data. Each branch of the dendrogram represents a cluster of samples that are closely related
based on their gene expression profiles. Samples that merge at lower distances are more
similar to each other than those that merge at higher distances. By examining the structure of
the dendrogram, we can identify clusters of patients with similar gene expression profiles, as
well as explore how subtypes of breast cancer are related to one another.
[Hierarchical Clustering]
2.5 Topological Data Analysis (TDA)
Topological Data Analysis (TDA) is an innovative method that focuses on capturing the "shape"
of data. Rather than looking for clusters based solely on distance, TDA examines the topological
features of the dataset, such as loops and voids, that persist across multiple scales. This
technique allows us to uncover higher-order relationships in the data that are not detectable by
traditional clustering methods.
The persistence diagram produced by TDA is shown in Figure 4. Each point in the diagram
represents a topological feature, with the x-axis representing the feature's "birth" (the scale at
which it first appears) and the y-axis representing its "death" (the scale at which it disappears).
Features that persist across a wide range of scales are considered more significant. In the
context of breast cancer gene expression data, these persistent features may correspond to
important biological processes that remain consistent across different cancer subtypes.
[Persistence Diagram]
2.6 Correlation Matrix
A Correlation Matrix provides a pairwise comparison of the expression levels of individual
genes. In this study, we generated a correlation matrix to explore relationships between genes,
such as co-expression and inverse expression. Genes that are strongly positively correlated are
likely to be involved in similar biological pathways, while genes with strong negative correlations
may represent opposing regulatory mechanisms.
The correlation matrix is shown in Figure 5, where red squares indicate positive correlations and
blue squares indicate negative correlations. By examining this matrix, we can identify groups of
co-expressed genes, which may reveal gene regulatory networks that drive tumor growth and
progression. This information is critical for identifying potential therapeutic targets and
understanding the molecular mechanisms underlying breast cancer.

The application of PCA and KMeans clustering revealed three distinct clusters of breast cancer
samples based on their gene expression profiles. As shown in Figure 2, these clusters are
visually separated, indicating that the samples within each cluster share similar gene expression
patterns. These clusters likely correspond to different molecular subtypes of breast cancer, each
with its own unique set of genetic drivers and biological characteristics. Identifying these
subtypes is crucial for developing targeted therapies that address the specific molecular
mechanisms of each subtype.
3.2 Hierarchical Clustering Results
The Hierarchical Clustering Dendrogram (Figure 3) offers a complementary view of the
relationships between breast cancer samples. The hierarchical structure allows us to explore
how closely related different samples are to each other and how these relationships evolve as
we move up or down the dendrogram. This structure helps us identify both broad subtypes and
finer-grained clusters of patients who share similar gene expression profiles. By examining the
hierarchical structure, we can gain a deeper understanding of how breast cancer subtypes are
related and potentially identify new subgroups within the data.
3.3 Persistence Diagram and TDA Insights
The Persistence Diagram (Figure 4) generated using TDA reveals several topological features
that persist across multiple scales. These persistent features represent higher-order
relationships between breast cancer samples that are not easily detected by traditional
clustering methods. The presence of such features suggests that they may correspond to
important biological processes or gene regulatory networks that are consistent across different
breast cancer subtypes. This novel approach provides insights that are difficult to capture using
traditional linear techniques like PCA or KMeans.
3.4 Correlation Matrix Insights
The Correlation Matrix (Figure 5) provides a visual representation of the relationships between
individual genes in the dataset. Several strong positive and negative correlations were
identified, indicating potential regulatory relationships between genes. For example, genes that
are positively correlated are likely to be co-regulated and may be involved in promoting tumor
cell proliferation, while negatively correlated genes may function as tumor suppressors.
Understanding these relationships is essential for identifying the gene regulatory networks that
drive tumor growth and progression, as well as for discovering potential targets for therapeutic
intervention.
4. Discussion
4.1 Implications of Clustering and Topological Data Analysis
The combination of PCA, KMeans clustering, hierarchical clustering, and TDA provides a
comprehensive approach to understanding the molecular diversity of breast cancer. The distinct
clusters identified through KMeans and hierarchical clustering suggest that breast cancer can
be subdivided into molecular subtypes with unique gene expression profiles. These subtypes
are likely to differ in their responses to treatment, making their identification critical for precision
medicine.
TDA adds an additional layer of complexity to our analysis by capturing higher-order
relationships that are not easily detected by traditional methods. By focusing on persistent
topological features, TDA provides new insights into the molecular mechanisms driving breast
cancer progression, which could inform the development of more targeted therapies.
4.2 Limitations and Future Directions
While this study provides valuable insights into the molecular drivers of breast cancer, it is not
without limitations. One limitation is the reliance on unsupervised learning techniques, which
may not capture all relevant patterns in the data. Additionally, the analysis was limited to a
specific dataset, and the findings may not be generalizable to other breast cancer populations.
Future research should focus on validating the identified clusters and topological features using
larger and more diverse datasets.

6. Python Sample
```python
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from sklearn.decomposition import PCA
from sklearn.cluster import KMeans
from scipy.cluster.hierarchy import dendrogram, linkage
import seaborn as sns
# Load dataset
data = np.load('breast_cancer_gene_expression_data.npy')
# Perform PCA
pca = PCA(n_components=2)
pca_result = pca.fit_transform(data)
# Visualize PCA result
plt.scatter(pca_result[:, 0], pca_result[:, 1], c='blue', s=50)
plt.title('PCA Visualization of Breast Cancer Gene Expression Data')
plt.xlabel('PCA Component 1')
plt.ylabel('PCA Component 2')
plt.show()
# Perform KMeans clustering
kmeans = KMeans(n_clusters=3)
clusters = kmeans.fit_predict(pca_result)
# Visualize KMeans clustering
plt.scatter(pca_result[:, 0], pca_result[:, 1], c=clusters, s=50, cmap='viridis')
plt.title('KMeans Clustering of Breast Cancer Gene Expression Data')
plt.xlabel('PCA Component 1')
plt.ylabel('PCA Component 2')
plt.show()
# Perform Hierarchical Clustering
Z = linkage(data, 'ward')
dendrogram(Z)
plt.title('Hierarchical Clustering Dendrogram')
plt.xlabel('Sample Index')
plt.ylabel('Distance')
plt.show()
# Generate Correlation Matrix
correlation_matrix = np.corrcoef(data)
sns.heatmap(correlation_matrix, cmap='coolwarm')
plt.title('Correlation Matrix of Gene Expression Data')
plt.show()
```
